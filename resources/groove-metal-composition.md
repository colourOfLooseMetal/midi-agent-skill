# Groove Metal Composition Guide

A practical reference for writing **groove metal** — the tightly-syncopated, mid-tempo
fusion of thrash metal's aggression with a locked-in rhythmic pocket, codified by
Pantera and carried into the modern era by **Lamb of God** and its peers. Written to be
usable directly from this MIDI skill (see *Rendering in this Skill*), but the theory
applies to any DAW, tab editor, or guitar in hand.

## What Makes Groove Metal Great

Groove metal is the sound of **precision as aggression**. Where thrash earns intensity
through raw speed, groove metal earns it by locking a riff into a pocket so tight that
the whole band moves like one organism. The appeal lives in a few specific pleasures:

- **The pocket is the hook.** It's in the name: *groove*. A great groove-metal riff
  isn't just heavy, it's syncopated in a way that makes your body want to move, not
  just your head bang. Off-beat accents and "missing" notes do as much work as the
  pitches themselves.
- **The gallop and the chug.** A vocabulary of palm-muted, percussive rhythm — rapid
  low chugging punctuated by ringing accent chords — that is instantly recognizable
  and endlessly reusable. This is the genre's riff-grammar in the same way the power
  chord is doom's.
- **Tightness as musicianship.** The whole point is that guitar, bass, and kick drum
  lock into the *exact* same rhythmic cell. Pantera built an entire identity on this
  (their "power groove"); Lamb of God's reputation rests on how precisely Chris
  Adler's kick tracks the guitars' picking pattern. The flex is ensemble precision,
  not solo chops.
- **The breakdown as payoff.** Verses build tension through syncopation and density;
  the breakdown resolves it by stripping down to a simplified, crushing, moshable
  riff. It's a destination the whole arrangement is built to earn.
- **Aggression with hooks.** Despite the brutality, riffs and choruses are often
  genuinely catchy — gang-shoutable, chantable. This distinguishes groove metal from
  pure technical/death metal, where density itself is the point.
- **A bridge genre.** It sits between thrash and everything that came after — nu
  metal, metalcore, and the New Wave of American Heavy Metal all inherited its DNA.
  Writing in this style means writing in the genre that taught modern metal how to
  groove.

The genius of the form is that it is **simple but unforgiving**: a single chug pattern
is easy to write, but making it lock, breathe, and pay off in a slam takes the same
discipline as a great drum part.

## The Lineage (Reference Points)

You cannot write convincingly in this style without internalizing where it comes from
and where it led — especially Lamb of God, the genre's modern flagbearer.

| Era | Artist / Album | What to steal |
|-----|----------------|----------------|
| Proto-groove | **Exhorder** – *Slaughter in the Vatican* (1990), **Prong**, **Bad Brains** (post-1987) | Thrash slowed and syncopated; hardcore's rhythmic directness bleeding into metal |
| The Codifier | **Pantera** – *Cowboys from Hell* (1990), *Vulgar Display of Power* (1992) | The gallop, the "power groove," pinch harmonics, Anselmo's rhythmic bark |
| Global popularizers | **Sepultura** – *Arise* / *Chaos A.D.*, **White Zombie**, **Machine Head** – *Burn My Eyes* (1994) | Tribal/percussive riffing, industrial-tinged groove, down-tuned aggression |
| Modern flagbearer | **Lamb of God** – *As the Palaces Burn* (2003), *Ashes of the Wake* (2004), *Sacrament* (2006) | Alternate-picked syncopation locked to the kick, Phrygian-dominant licks, hardcore-rooted vocal phrasing |
| Technical/atmospheric wing | **Gojira** – *From Mars to Sirius*, *Magma* | Polyrhythm, pick-scrape texture, riffs built from drum patterns first |
| Second-wave 2000s | **DevilDriver**, **Chimaira** | Modern production, groove metal fused directly with metalcore breakdown structure |

A fact worth knowing: Pantera's signature **gallop** rhythm — the "long-short-short"
double-bass figure under songs like "Psycho Holiday" — started as drummer Vinnie Paul
taking the shuffle feel of Van Halen's "Hot for Teacher" and playing it in half-time.
A borrowed groove, slowed down, became one of metal's most copied rhythms.

## Core Ingredients: Tempo, Tuning, Tone

### Tempo
Mid-paced and felt as a **pocket**, not a sprint. The key difference from thrash is
that groove metal modulates *feel* — via drum density and subdivision — far more than
it modulates raw BPM. A song can hold one tempo all the way through and still feel
"slow" in the breakdown and "fast" in the verse.

| Feel | BPM | Notes |
|------|-----|-------|
| Half-time breakdown feel | 70–100 (felt) | Often the *same underlying tempo* as the verse, just played in half-time |
| The groove pocket (default) | 100–140 | The genre's sweet spot — thrash DNA at a walking pace |
| Gallop / verse drive | 140–180 | Where the thrash heritage shows through hardest |

### Tuning
Down-tuned, but for percussive punch rather than droning weight.

| Tuning | Lowest string | Used by / vibe |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Standard E | E2 | Early, *Cowboys from Hell*-era Pantera, before the down-tuning |
| Drop D (DADGBE) | D2 | **Lamb of God's constant** — one-finger power chords, the modern default |
| Whole-step-down standard (DGCFAD) | D2 | Dimebag Darrell from *Vulgar Display of Power* onward |
| Drop B / B standard | B1 | Later Sepultura — heavier, more extreme low end |

For MIDI you don't tune anything — you **write riffs in a low octave** (roots around
**D2** is the idiomatic groove-metal analog of Drop D) and pick a tight, percussive
patch. See *Rendering in this Skill*.

### Tone (precision over wash)
- **High-gain but tight.** A noise gate is doing real compositional work here — chugs
  need to start and stop cleanly, not bleed into the next note the way doom's fuzz is
  allowed to.
- **Present mids, not a full scoop.** The fully-scooped "metal" tone is a cliché that
  loses the percussive "bark" this genre depends on to cut through a mix.
- **Pinch harmonics** as a deliberate, sparse accent — a vocal-sounding squeal on a
  held note, Dimebag Darrell's signature move.
- **Punchy, modern production.** The opposite end of the spectrum from stoner doom's
  lo-fi warmth: tight low end, controlled transients, clarity on the kick and chug.

## Harmony & Scales (Blues Bones, Phrygian-Dominant Edge)

Groove metal shares doom's blues-rooted minor backbone but reaches further for exotic
color, and uses chromatic motion actively rather than as a rare accent.

### Foundation scales
- **Minor Pentatonic / Blues scale** — `R, ♭3, 4, (♭5), 5, ♭7`. The same blue-collar
  backbone as most heavy riffing, but used percussively here — short cells, not long
  bends.
- **Phrygian** — `R, ♭2, ♭3, 4, 5, ♭6, ♭7`. Dark, half-step grind above the root.
- **Phrygian Dominant** — `R, ♭2, 3, 4, 5, ♭6, ♭7` (the 5th mode of harmonic minor).
  This is the **Lamb of God color**: Mark Morton has cited Marty Friedman's "exotic
  metal" playing as the source of his love for this scale. The natural (not flatted)
  **3rd** combined with the **♭2** gives a Middle-Eastern/"snake-charmer" flavor that
  regular Phrygian doesn't have — it's the single easiest way to make a riff sound
  identifiably *this* genre rather than generic minor-key metal.

### Practical harmony rules
- **Power chords, played percussively.** Root + 5th (7 semitones), often + octave —
  but struck short and muted, not left to ring, except on accent/release hits.
- **Chromatic crawl is a feature, not a cheat.** Sliding a power-chord *shape* down by
  half-steps while keeping the rhythm constant is the breakdown's signature move (see
  *Rendering in this Skill* for why this doesn't break the skill's dissonance rule).
- **The ♭7 "drop" stab.** Chugging on the root, then slamming a chord a whole step
  down on the ♭7, is everywhere in this genre's vocabulary — an "answer" to the root
  that still feels like home.
- **Pedal point, but active.** Unlike doom's slow drone, the pedal root here is
  constantly re-struck (the chug itself) rather than sustained as one long note.

## Riff Construction

The rhythmic cell is the unit of composition here — pitch is often secondary to *where
the notes land*.

### Anatomy of a great groove riff
1. **A rhythmic cell first.** Decide the chug pattern before you decide the notes.
2. **A chug** — a repeated, palm-muted low note that establishes the pocket.
3. **A syncopated push or pull** — an accent that lands off the beat you'd expect.
4. **A release** — an open power-chord slam or pinch harmonic that pays off the
   tension the muted chug built up.
5. **Tight, fast-mutating repetition.** Unlike doom's hypnotic 8–16 repeats of one
   riff, groove-metal riffs typically cycle in 1–2 bar units and mutate every pass or
   two — more riffs per song, less time spent on each.

### The gallop (the genre's signature rhythm cell)
A beat subdivided "long-short-short" — historically a half-timed shuffle. Two gallop
cells fill the first half of a bar; the second half releases into a slam:

```
Beat:     1   +   2   +   3       4
Pattern:  ──gallop──  ──gallop──  ════ slam ════
Feel:     chug-chug-CHUG  chug-chug-CHUG   mute released, power chord rings
```

### Syncopation as "the missing note"
Lamb of God's rhythm tricks work by starting from a straight subdivision and removing
notes — the gap is what creates the lurch. Each removed note becomes a longer note in
its place:

| Pattern | Feel |
|---------|------|
| Straight 16ths (no syncopation) | even, predictable |
| Merge notes 1–2 (the **gallop**) | "long-short-short" — the classic forward gallop |
| Merge notes 2–3 (mid-beat lurch) | the accent lands late-mid-beat |
| Merge notes 3–4 (**reverse gallop**) | the chord rings late, bleeding into the next beat |

### Riff-writing techniques
- **Lock the chug to where a kick would fall.** Even without real drums, write the
  rhythm as if a kick drum is doubling it note-for-note — that imagined lock is what
  makes a riff feel "groove metal" rather than just fast.
- **Pinch harmonics as accents**, sparingly, on release hits.
- **Chromatic crawl** for breakdowns and transitions — slide the chord shape, keep the
  rhythm.
- **Call-and-response** between a low chug and a higher stab or pinch harmonic.
- **Odd meter and polyrhythm as a hook, not a garnish** (the Gojira approach) — unlike
  doom, where odd meter is a rare spice, here it can be the riff's whole identity.

### What to avoid in a riff
Constant tremolo with no rhythmic shape; one chug pattern for an entire song with no
accent or variation; scalar noodling (that's tech-death/metalcore territory, not
groove). The rhythm needs a clear "where it lands," every time.

## Song Structure & Arrangement

Groove metal is **riff-chain, not riff-monolith** — more sections, faster turnover,
shorter songs than doom.

### Common forms
- **Verse/chorus/breakdown hybrid** — the most common modern structure; the chorus is
  the catchiest riff, the breakdown is the heaviest.
- **The riff-chain** (classic Pantera) — a sequence of distinct riffs (verse A, verse
  B, chorus, bridge) rather than one riff explored at length.
- **The cold open.** Many songs in this genre start with guitar (and maybe bass) alone
  stating the main chug before the full band slams in on the gallop — an instant
  genre signal.

### A reliable arrangement template
```
1. Cold-open riff    — guitar/bass alone state the chug, 1–2 bars
2. Full slam-in      — drums hit, same riff now locked to the gallop
3. Verse riff        — tight, syncopated, leaves room for vocals
4. Pre-chorus lift   — register and density rise, often straighter rhythm
5. Chorus / hook     — the most "open," gang-shoutable riff
6. Verse 2           — variation on verse riff
7. Breakdown         — simplified chug, chromatic crawl, half-time feel
8. Solo / lead break — over the verse riff or a static vamp
9. Final chorus      — heaviest restatement
10. Outro stab       — a hard, abrupt cutoff, not a fade
```

### Arrangement principles
- **Economy.** Most songs run 3–5 minutes — say it with riff turnover, not repetition.
- **The breakdown is the destination.** Build the whole arrangement to earn it.
- **Abrupt endings are idiomatic.** A hard final stab, not a decay into feedback —
  the opposite instinct from doom.

## The Rhythm Section (Drums + Bass)

### Drums — the gallop is a drum part as much as a guitar part
The kick drum traces the riff's rhythm; it isn't just timekeeping underneath it.

A basic groove-metal gallop-and-slam pattern in 4/4:
```
Beat:    1   e   +   a   2   e   +   a   3       4
Kick:    X   .   X   X   X   .   X   X   X       X       (the gallop: long-short-short ×2)
Snare:   .   .   .   .   .   .   .   .   X       .       (slam landing on 3)
Crash:   .   .   .   .   .   .   .   .   X       .       (accents the release)
```
- **Kick locked to the guitar's chug**, note-for-note — this synchronization (Chris
  Adler's reputation is built on it) is the genre's core discipline.
- **Crash accents on the release**, not on every downbeat.
- **Blast-beat bursts as an occasional extreme accent** (the Gojira/modern wing), used
  sparingly — constant blasting is a different genre (grind/death).
- **Snare drives the breakdown's half-time backbeat**, big and simple, once the riff
  strips down.

### Bass — locked, not divergent
- **Note-for-note with the guitar's muted root.** Unlike doom, where the bass can peel
  off into a droning lead role, here it reinforces the same rhythmic engine.
- **Picked, percussive, present in the mix** — it should cut, not just be felt.

## Vocals & Lyrics

- **Style:** Aggressive — barked/shouted (Phil Anselmo's rhythmic bark) or a
  hardcore-rooted scream/growl hybrid (Randy Blythe, who cites hardcore/grind
  vocalists Lee Dorrian and Kevin Sharp as his major influences, and is known for
  enunciating clearly even through a scream).
- **Phrasing is rhythmic, not melodic.** Vocals sync tightly to the riff's syncopation
  — short, punchy, percussive phrases that land *with* the chug, not over a flowing
  line. This is the opposite instinct from stoner doom's sustained, behind-the-beat
  drawl.
- **Themes:** Anger with a target — political and war critique (Lamb of God's
  *Ashes of the Wake* attacks the Iraq War and the Bush administration directly),
  crime and street life, personal rage, anti-authority defiance. Direct and pointed,
  rarely mythic or cosmic the way doom's lyrics are.
- **Gang vocals on the hook.** Chorus lines are often written to be shouted back by a
  crowd — the hook needs to be chantable, not just sung.

## Tone & Production

- **Tight and gated**, not loose and washy — the chug needs silence between mutes in
  a real recording. You can approximate that gating here by punching short rests
  (`pitch "R"`) between chug hits for a staccato cut (see *Rests and the chug*),
  though MIDI still won't capture a real gate's transient shaping.
- **Present mids over a full scoop.** The "bark" that lets a chug cut through a dense
  mix lives in the midrange — don't scoop it away.
- **Punchy, controlled low end.** Tight, not boomy — the opposite of doom's loose,
  room-filling bass weight.
- **Bright, present drums.** You need to hear the gallop's subdivisions clearly; a
  washy, reverb-soaked kit buries the genre's whole point.
- **Modern clarity over vintage warmth.** Where stoner doom wants you to *feel*
  analog warmth, groove metal wants precision you can hear.

## Rendering in this MIDI Skill

This repo represents music as `Composition(title, bpm, tracks[])`, where each `Track`
has a GM `instrument` key and a list of `Note(pitch, duration)`. See
[music.py](../midi_types/music.py) and [gm_instruments.py](../midi_types/gm_instruments.py).
Groove metal's defining techniques — syncopation, palm-muted chugging, breakdowns
that drop instruments out — map onto this skill cleanly. **Per-note `velocity` and
single-track chords now exist**, so accents and power chords are direct; the one
remaining friction is **no real drum kit**. (Rests are available too: a note with
`pitch "R"` is true silence — see *Rests and the chug* below.) The translations below
are tested against the actual generator code, not just the theory above.

### Instrument palette (GM keys that exist in this repo)
| Role | Instrument key | Notes |
|------|----------------|-------|
| Chug / rhythm guitar | `electric-guitar-muted` | GM program 28 — a built-in palm-mute timbre. The single best instrument-level trick for this genre; nothing comparable existed for the stoner-doom guide |
| Release / lead guitar | `distortion-guitar` or `overdriven-guitar` | Full-gain, ringing — for slam chords and solos |
| Harmonic accent | `guitar-harmonics` | Pinch-harmonic squeals as a sparse top layer |
| Bass | `electric-bass-pick` | Picked = percussive attack, matches the genre's tight low end |
| Drums | *(none available)* | Same hard constraint as the rest of this skill: `generate_midi.py` actively skips channel 9, and the `drums` alias resolves to `synth-drum` (GM program 118), a pitched melodic voice, not a kit. Build the gallop's identity into the guitar/bass chug patterns themselves — there is no path to a real kit here. |

### Rests and the chug: sustain *or* silence
You have two ways to put a syncopated "gap" in a riff, and they sound different —
pick deliberately:

- **Sustain (a longer note in place of the removed one).** Real palm-muted chugging
  isn't silent; a struck note rings briefly into where the next one would have been.
  Model that by **lengthening the surviving note** — the attack still lands off the
  beat, with no true gap before it. This is the right default for a *ringing*,
  locked-in chug.
- **A true rest (`pitch "R"`).** A note with pitch `R` (also `rest`/`-`) produces
  actual silence for its duration (`generate_midi.py` advances `time` without sounding
  it). Reach for this when you want a **staccato gap** — the note genuinely stops, the
  way a hard-gated, tightly-muted chug cuts dead between hits — rather than ringing.

Concretely, starting from straight 16ths (`16,16,16,16`, 1 beat). The "sustain" column
lengthens the kept note; the "rest" column drops in an `R` of the removed note's value
for an audible cut instead:

| Technique | Sustain (longer note) | True gap (rest) | Result |
|-----------|-----------------------|-----------------|--------|
| No notes removed | `16,16,16,16` | `16,16,16,16` | straight, no syncopation |
| Remove note 2 (**the gallop**) | `8,16,16` | `16,R16,16,16` | "long-short-short" forward gallop |
| Remove note 3 | `16,8,16` | `16,16,R16,16` | mid-beat lurch |
| Remove note 4 (**reverse gallop**) | `16,16,8` | `16,16,16,R16` | the gap lands late in the beat |

(`R16` = a sixteenth-note rest: `{"pitch": "R", "duration": "16"}`.) Every row sums to
exactly 1.0 beat either way — they're drop-in substitutes for each other in a bar.
Sustain reads heavier and more "ringing"; the rest reads tighter and more percussive.

### A worked gallop-and-slam riff (D, one bar of 4/4)
A muted chug usually doesn't need power-chord layering in real playing either — a
palm-muted note is too short and dead to need harmony. **Layer the power chord only
on the release/slam**, where the harmony actually has room to speak:

```python
Note("D2", "8")    # chug — root, muted
Note("D2", "16")   # chug
Note("D2", "16")   # chug — gallop cell 1 complete (beat 1)
Note("D2", "8")    # chug
Note("D2", "16")   # chug
Note("D2", "16")   # chug — gallop cell 2 complete (beat 2)
Note("C2", "2")    # slam on the ♭7 (2 semitones below D2) — mute released, half note
```
That's the `electric-guitar-muted` track (the chug stays single-note — a palm-mute
needs no harmony). The slam's power chord goes in **one `distortion-guitar` track as a
stacked-pitch chord** — `"C2+G2+C3"` (root, +5th, +octave) — not three parallel layer
tracks. The cleanest way to make the slam *open up* is to have that chord track **rest
through the chug and enter on the release**:

```python
{"pitch": "R", "duration": "1"}                         # silent through the two gallop cells
{"pitch": "C2+G2+C3", "duration": "2", "velocity": 120} # slam blooms in on the release
```

The chord voice is genuinely silent through the chug and slams in only on the release.
Double the sounding root with the bass an octave down (its own track). Use a separate
guitar track *only* when you want a different timbre or rhythm — not just to stack a
chord.

### Accenting with velocity
Per-note `velocity` (1–127, default 100) now does the loud/soft directly: keep the
gated chug lower (~85–95, with light downbeat accents) and hit the slam chords hard
(~118–122) so they explode out of the mute. Reinforce it with **duration contrast** (a
sudden half-note slam after a string of identical 16th chugs reads as an accent from
contour too) — velocity and contour together make the dynamic unmistakable.

### The breakdown: silence, subtraction, and the crawl
Rests make real subtraction possible. A track can go **silent and come back**: insert
a rest (`pitch "R"`) of the right length where the voice drops out, then resume with
notes — the held silence puts that instrument's later notes back in sync with the rest
of the band (every track shares one timeline from `time = 0`, and a rest just advances
that track's cursor). The same trick stages a **cold open**: lead a track with a rest
so it enters a bar or two after the guitar's lone chug — the genre's signature "guitar
alone, then the band slams in."

**One caveat, verified against the generator: a rest is only realized if a note
follows it later in the same track.** Leading rests (a late entrance) and interior rests
(a gap, a drop-out-and-return) both render as true silence because there's a note after
them. But a *trailing* rest — one with no subsequent note in its track — is silently
dropped: the track's end-of-track marker lands on its last *note*, not on the advanced
cursor. This is harmless for audio (silence after a track's final note is
indistinguishable from the track simply ending — exactly what you want for an abrupt
outro stab), but don't rely on a trailing rest to pad a track to a target length, add a
measured gap of silence after the final hit, or hold two tracks to equal length for a
downstream tool — it won't survive into the file.

Two complementary breakdown moves, then:
- **Subtract.** Rest the harmony/lead layers out entirely and leave a single crushing
  chug exposed, then bring them back on the heavy restatement. (You can still also just
  end a track's note list early if a voice should drop out and *never* return — tracks
  may differ in length.)
- **Crawl.** Collapse the surviving voices onto a slow, chromatically-crawling chug —
  same chord shape, same rhythm, sliding down by a half-step every bar or two. **This
  does not violate this skill's "never play notes 1 semitone apart" dissonance rule** —
  that rule governs notes sounding *simultaneously across tracks*, not consecutive
  notes within one track's timeline. A chromatic descent in time is exactly how real
  breakdowns are written. Keep the single-track power-chord *shape* (`"Dn+An+Dn+1"` =
  root, +7, +12) identical at every step so transposing the whole chord down preserves
  the same intervals — and therefore the same consonance — throughout the crawl.

### Polyrhythm and odd meter
The generator now writes a `time_signature` (default `"4/4"`) meta-event, so you *can*
notate `"7/8"` and have the bar structure land in the file. But the meter feel doesn't
*depend* on that field — it comes from the durations:
- **Odd-meter feel** is just a loop whose total duration isn't a multiple of 4 beats —
  write a phrase that sums to, say, 7 beats and repeat it; the asymmetry against the
  surrounding pulse *is* the odd-meter effect (set `time_signature: "7/8"` to match).
- **True polyrhythm is easy and exact.** Since every track is just a continuous stream
  of absolute time from `t=0`, give two tracks duration-cycles of different lengths
  that share the same BPM: a guitar phrase totaling 7 beats, looped, against a bass
  phrase totaling 4 beats, looped. They drift and realign every 28 beats — a genuine
  7-over-4 polyrhythm (the Gojira technique), and the engine renders it more reliably
  than a human rhythm section would.

### Settings checklist
- **BPM:** `110`–`150` for the main groove. You can drop into a half-time breakdown
  either by changing note *density and duration* or with a real `tempo_map` entry —
  both work; density contrast alone is often enough.
- **Octave:** write roots around **D2** (the Drop-D analog) or **B1** (drop-B).
- **Durations:** favor `8`/`16` combinations for the chug, `2`/`1` for release/slam
  hits. **Stick to documented tokens** — `parse_duration()` only recognizes the exact
  strings in `DURATION_MAP` (`1`, `2`, `d2`, `4`, `d4`, `8`, `d8`, `16`, `32`, `T4`,
  `T8`) or a bare number `N` (→ a `1/N` note); anything else, including a plausible-
  looking `d16` or `T16`, silently falls through to a quarter note with no error.
- **Rests:** use `pitch "R"` (also `rest`/`-`) with any normal duration for true
  silence — staccato gaps, cold-open entrances, and subtraction breakdowns.
- **Track budget:** max 15 melodic tracks total (channel 9 is skipped). Single-track
  chords keep this in check — a typical arrangement is just muted chug, chordal slam
  guitar, bass, and a sparse harmonics accent (4 tracks), not a stack of layer tracks.

## Quick-Start Recipe

1. Pick a tonal center and write a riff on the **Phrygian-dominant** or **blues**
   scale, root around **D2**, BPM **110–140**.
2. Build a **gallop cell**: `8,16,16` on the muted root (`electric-guitar-muted`),
   repeated to fill most of the bar.
3. End the bar with a **release**: a half-note power chord as one stacked pitch
   (`"C2+G2+C3"`) on `distortion-guitar`, landing on the root or a ♭7 drop, mute
   released, with a hard `velocity` (~120) so it slams.
4. **Lock a bass track** to the same rhythm, doubling whichever root is sounding an
   octave down.
5. Vary the riff every 1–2 cycles — don't let it sit as long as a doom riff would.
6. Write a **breakdown**: collapse everything to the chug rhythm with a chromatic
   half-step crawl, same chord shape throughout.
7. Optional: a `guitar-harmonics` track with sparse, held notes for pinch-harmonic
   accents on release hits.
8. **End on a hard stab**, not a fade.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Too smooth.** Legato runs and long sustains read as doom or prog, not groove —
  lean on duration contrast since this engine has no articulation control.
- **No release.** A chug with nothing to resolve into is just monotone noise — every
  gallop needs a slam.
- **Over-syncopating into mush.** If every beat is "missing" a note, there's no pulse
  left to syncopate *against*. Keep a clear underlying grid.
- **The full-scoop tone cliché.** Keep mids present in your instrument/voicing choices
  the way you would in a real mix.
- **Treating the breakdown as an afterthought.** It's the destination — build toward it.
- **Inventing duration tokens.** `d16`, `T16`, `T32` etc. aren't in `DURATION_MAP` and
  silently become quarter notes — verify against the documented token list.
- **Expecting a drum kit.** There isn't one in this skill; the gallop's identity has
  to live entirely in the guitar and bass tracks.
