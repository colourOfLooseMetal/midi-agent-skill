# Stoner Doom Composition Guide

A practical reference for writing **stoner doom metal** — the fusion of traditional
doom's crushing weight with stoner rock's fuzzy, psychedelic groove. Written to be
usable directly from this MIDI skill (see *Rendering in this Skill*), but the theory
applies to any DAW, tab editor, or guitar in hand.

## What Makes Stoner Doom Great

Stoner doom is the sound of **patience as heaviness**. Where most metal earns
intensity through speed, stoner doom earns it by slowing down until a single
chord becomes an event. The appeal lives in a few specific pleasures:

- **The riff is the song.** Melody, harmony, and hook are all collapsed into one
  fat, repeated guitar figure. A great stoner doom riff is a groove you can live
  inside, not a passage you pass through. This is "riff worship" — the riff is
  treated as a sacred object and earns the right to repeat 8, 16, 32 times.
- **Weight you can feel.** Down-tuned strings, fuzz-saturated amps, and a bass
  that moves real air give the music physical mass. At slow tempos every note
  has room to bloom, distort, and decay — the *space* is as important as the notes.
- **Hypnosis.** Repetition + slow tempo + drone = trance. The music stops being a
  sequence of events and becomes a *state*. This is the "psychedelic" half of the
  genre's DNA: it's heavy metal you can meditate to.
- **Groove over chops.** It swings. Rooted in the blues and in early Black Sabbath,
  stoner doom prioritizes feel, swagger, and pocket over technical display. It is
  heavy music you can nod your head to.
- **Dynamics through restraint.** Drop to just bass and drums, let it breathe, then
  bring the wall of guitar back in — the contrast hits harder than any blast beat.
- **A whole aesthetic world.** Vintage/analog warmth, occult and cosmic and
  cannabis imagery, fuzzed-out album art, and an unhurried, defiant refusal to
  hurry up. The vibe is a feature, not decoration.

The genius of the form is that it is **simple but not easy**: with three notes and
a fuzz pedal you have everything you need, but making three notes hypnotic instead
of boring is the entire craft.

## The Lineage (Reference Points)

You cannot write convincingly in this style without internalizing where it comes
from. Black Sabbath invented the vocabulary; everyone since has dialected it.

| Era | Artist / Album | What to steal |
|-----|----------------|---------------|
| The Big Bang | **Black Sabbath** – *Master of Reality* (1971) | Down-tuning, the tritone, slow blues-metal riffs, weed/sci-fi themes |
| Traditional anchor | **Saint Vitus**, **Pentagram**, **Trouble** | Keeping the Sabbath flame; doom as a feel, not a tempo |
| Stoner birth | **Kyuss** – *Blues for the Red Sun* (1992), *Welcome to Sky Valley* (1994) | Desert groove, guitars through bass amps, psychedelic heaviness |
| Doom monolith | **Sleep** – *Sleep's Holy Mountain* (1992), *Dopesmoker* (2003) | The single-riff epic; *Dopesmoker* is one ~63-minute track |
| Maximum heavy | **Electric Wizard** – *Dopethrone* (2000) | Lo-fi murk, ultra-downtuned filth, horror/occult atmosphere |
| Trad-leaning | **Cathedral** – *Forest of Equilibrium* (1991) | Glacial tempos, doom as dread |
| Meditative fringe | **Om**, **Acid King**, **Ufomammut**, **Windhand**, **Yob** | Drone, ritual, the bass-as-lead, atmosphere |

Foundational fact worth knowing: Tony Iommi lost the tips of two fretting fingers
in a factory accident, so he **detuned and used light strings** to ease the
tension. Down-tuning — the single defining trait of the entire genre — started as
an ergonomic accident and became the sound of heavy music.

## Core Ingredients: Tempo, Tuning, Tone

### Tempo
Slow, and usually felt in **half-time** (the "big" backbeat lands on beat 3 of a
slow 4/4 rather than beats 2 and 4).

| Feel | BPM | Notes |
|------|-----|-------|
| Groovy / stoner-leaning | 90–110 | The Kyuss desert-rock end; still nods |
| Classic stoner doom | 60–85 | The sweet spot — heavy but it grooves |
| Pure doom crush | 50–65 | Each chord is an event; verges on funeral doom |

When in doubt, write it slower than feels natural, then go slower still. Long note
values (halves and wholes) let fuzz and feedback do the work.

### Tuning
Down-tuned, always. Standard tuning is too bright and too tense to sound heavy.

| Tuning | Lowest string | Used by / vibe |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| C# standard (−3) | C#2 | Black Sabbath's original heavy sound |
| Drop C (CGCFAD) | C2 | One-finger power chords, modern stoner doom default |
| C standard (−4) | C2 | Sabbath-worship sweet spot |
| B standard / Drop B (−5) | B1 | Heavier, sludgier |
| A / drop A and below | A1 | Electric Wizard / extreme territory |

For MIDI you don't *tune* anything — you simply **write the riffs in a low octave**
(roots around **C2–C3**) and pick a heavy patch. See *Rendering in this Skill*.

### Tone (the third instrument)
Tone is compositional in this genre. The non-negotiables:

- **Fuzz** — the defining texture. Big Muff, Fuzz Face, Sunn-style. Thick, woolly,
  saturated.
- **Cranked tube amp** with heavy low end; mids present enough to cut (fully
  scooped mids disappear in a mix — see *Pitfalls*).
- **Reverb & delay** for space and psychedelic depth, especially on leads.
- **Feedback** used deliberately as an instrument/texture, not an accident.
- Guitars often run through **bass cabs** for extra girth (the Kyuss trick).

## Harmony & Scales (The Four Intervals of Doom)

Stoner doom is harmonically simple and **minor-key**. Build everything on the
**Natural Minor scale (Aeolian)** and color it with a small set of dark intervals.
You rarely need more than a handful of notes.

### Foundation scales
- **Minor Pentatonic** — `R, ♭3, 4, 5, ♭7`. The bluesy backbone of most riffs.
- **Minor Blues** — pentatonic **+ the ♭5 (tritone)**: `R, ♭3, 4, ♭5, 5, ♭7`. The
  most versatile single scale for the genre. The ♭5 is the money note.
- **Natural Minor (Aeolian)** — `R, 2, ♭3, 4, 5, ♭6, ♭7`. The harmonic anchor.
- **Phrygian** — natural minor with a **♭2**: `R, ♭2, ♭3, 4, 5, ♭6, ♭7`. Reach for
  it when you want maximum dread/evil.

### The Four Intervals of Doom
Over a minor tonic, these are the spices. Sprinkle them onto a minor framework and
the riff will sound "correct" *and* heavy:

| Interval | Effect | How to use |
|----------|--------|------------|
| **♭5 — Tritone** | The signature doom sound; tense, "evil," unresolved | The classic Iommi move: root → up to the tritone and let it hang |
| **♭2 — Minor 2nd** | The most dissonant interval in Western music; pure unease | Phrygian color; a half-step grind above the root |
| **maj7 (♮7)** | Moderately dissonant in a minor context | As a passing tone leading up to the octave |
| **maj3 over minor** | Sounds intentionally "wrong/ugly" against the minor key | An advanced, knowing dissonance — use sparingly |

The **perfect 5th** is the other essential interval — but as *consonance*: it's the
power chord (root + 5th, often + octave) that the whole genre is built on. Tension
(tritone/♭2) against the stability of the power chord is the core push-pull.

### Practical harmony rules
- **Power chords, not triads.** Root+5th (intervals of 7 semitones), frequently with
  the octave on top (root, +7, +12). Major/minor thirds are usually left out of the
  chord and implied by the riff's single-note runs instead.
- **Pedal the root.** Keep returning to a droning low root note between moves.
- **Don't resolve everything.** Leaving a phrase hanging on the tritone or ♭2 is the
  point. Tension *is* the aesthetic.
- **One key, one drone.** Many great songs never leave the home key. A static drone
  underneath shifting riffs creates the hypnotic effect.

## Riff Construction

The riff is the unit of composition. A complete stoner doom song can be one great
riff and its variations.

### Anatomy of a great riff
1. **A strong, simple motif** — memorable in 2–4 notes. If you can't hum it, simplify.
2. **A low pedal root** the riff keeps falling back to.
3. **One dark color note** (the tritone or ♭2) for tension.
4. **Big note values** — let chords ring; resist filling every beat.
5. **Groove/swing** — where the notes sit in the pocket matters more than how many.

### The classic Iommi shape (in C minor)
Root, up a minor 3rd, up to the tritone, hang, then fall home:

```
Beat:   1   +   2   +   3   +   4   +
Riff:   C2 ----- Eb2 --- Gb2 ------- (let the tritone ring)
        |  root  |  ♭3   |   ♭5 / tritone — tension
Resolve next bar:  F2 -- Eb2 -- C2 -------- (whole note home)
```

### Riff-writing techniques
- **Repetition with variation.** State the riff; repeat it; on the 4th pass change
  the tail. Let it cycle long enough to become hypnotic before you move on.
- **Call-and-response.** A heavy low phrase "answered" by a higher fill or a
  feedback swell.
- **Palm-mute then release.** Chug the muted low root, then slam an open, ringing
  power chord — the contrast is the dynamic.
- **Slow bends & slides** for bluesy, vocal phrasing (a half- or whole-step bend
  on a sustained note adds enormous expression at slow tempo).
- **Pinch harmonics** as screaming accents on held notes.
- **The "even slower" trick.** Take the main riff and play it at half speed (double
  the note values) for the heaviest section of the song.
- **Odd meter, used sparingly.** A 7/8 or 6/8 riff or a dropped beat can make a
  groove feel lurching and off-balance — effective in small doses.

### What to avoid in a riff
Too many notes, too fast, no repetition, and resolving the tension immediately.
Commit to the riff — restlessness is the enemy of the trance.

## Song Structure & Arrangement

Stoner doom is **riff-based, not necessarily verse/chorus**. Structures range from
conventional to monolithic.

### Common forms
- **Riff-cycle / monolith** — one or two riffs explored at length, with dynamic
  builds and drops. Sleep's *Dopesmoker* is the extreme: a single ~63-minute track.
- **Loose verse/chorus** — a trad-doom skeleton where the "chorus" is just the
  biggest riff (Cathedral, some Electric Wizard).
- **The jam** — a psychedelic mid-song section that opens up for soloing/drone
  before slamming back into the main riff.

### A reliable arrangement template
```
1. Intro        — feedback, a clean/lead figure, or a slow fade-in of the main riff
2. Main riff    — the monster, stated and cycled (let it breathe: 4–8 repeats)
3. Variation A  — same riff, new tail / added vocal
4. Breakdown    — strip to bass + drums; build tension; OR drop to the "even slower" riff
5. Psych jam    — solo / drone / wah over the drone; reverb and delay open up the space
6. The return   — main riff comes back heavier; often the heaviest moment of the song
7. Outro        — let it decay into feedback and cymbal wash
```

### Arrangement principles
- **Length is allowed.** 7–20+ minute songs are normal. Earn the length with
  dynamics, not just duration.
- **Dynamics = the only "fast" thing.** Your loud/quiet, full/sparse contrasts do
  the work that tempo does in other metal.
- **Subtraction is a tool.** Dropping the guitar out and coming back is your biggest
  weapon. Negative space makes the wall of fuzz feel bigger.

## The Rhythm Section (Drums + Bass)

### Drums — heavy, simple, spacious
The drummer's job is *weight and pocket*, not flash. Sit slightly behind the beat.

Half-time doom groove in 4/4 (the genre default — note the snare on beat 3):
```
Beat:    1   +   2   +   3   +   4   +
Crash:   X   .   .   .   .   .   .   .     (accent the downbeat of a riff)
Kick:    X   .   .   X   .   .   .   .
Snare:   .   .   .   .   X   .   .   .     (the big backbeat)
Ride:    X   .   X   .   X   .   X   .     (sparse; let cymbals wash and ring)
```

- **Big, open hits.** Let crashes and the ride wash and decay.
- **Lots of space.** Avoid busy fills; a single slow tom roll into the main riff is
  worth more than a flurry.
- **No (or minimal) double bass.** Blast beats and rapid double-kick are the wrong
  genre — they kill the trance.
- **Tom-heavy fills** and cymbal swells suit the primal, tribal feel.

### Bass — the foundation (and sometimes the lead)
- **Fuzzed and fat.** The bass is felt as much as heard; run it with grit.
- **Lock to the riff,** doubling the guitar root for maximum mass — but it can
  diverge to hold the drone while the guitar moves, or carry the riff alone during
  guitar drop-outs.
- In the most minimal corners of the genre (Om, early Sleep moments) the bass is the
  **lead instrument** and the song's center of gravity.

## Vocals & Lyrics

- **Style:** Usually **clean and melodic** — the wailing, mid-range, Ozzy-descended
  voice — drenched in reverb and frequently sitting *inside* the mix rather than on
  top. Chant-like and repetitive to reinforce the trance. (Sludgier cousins use a
  harsh roar; that's the sludge-doom border.)
- **Phrasing:** Long, sustained, behind-the-beat. The voice is another slow,
  hypnotic layer, not a busy melodic showcase.
- **Lyrics/themes:** Cannabis and ritual, wizards and the occult, cosmic/sci-fi
  voyages, deserts and pilgrimage, doom and dread, and self-aware riff-worship.
  Imagery is heavy, mythic, and unhurried.
- **It's fine to be mostly instrumental.** Many classics are riff-driven with sparse
  vocals; the riff carries the song.

## Tone & Production

Production is part of the writing — these choices shape what you compose.

- **Fuzz first.** Saturated, woolly fuzz on guitar (and often bass) is the genre's
  signature texture.
- **"Retro"/analog warmth.** Aim for vintage, slightly raw production rather than
  modern clinical clarity. Some lo-fi murk is a feature (see *Dopethrone*).
- **Low-end heavy, mids present.** Boosted bass and a moderate scoop — but keep
  enough mids to cut through. Fully scooped mids vanish in a full mix.
- **Reverb & delay** for depth and psychedelia, especially on leads and vocals.
- **Feedback & drone** as deliberate connective tissue between sections.
- **Loud as an aesthetic.** The music is conceived to be played at volume; write
  with sustain and decay in mind.

## Rendering in this MIDI Skill

This repo represents music as `Composition(title, bpm, tracks[])`, where each
`Track` has a GM `instrument` key and a list of `Note(pitch, duration)` — pitches
like `"C2"`, durations like `"1"` (whole), `"2"` (half), `"4"` (quarter), `"8"`
(eighth). See [music.py](../midi_types/music.py) and
[gm_instruments.py](../midi_types/gm_instruments.py).

### Instrument palette (GM keys that exist in this repo)
| Role | Instrument key | Notes |
|------|----------------|-------|
| Main riff guitar | `distortion-guitar` | The wall of fuzz; voice each note as a `root+5th+octave` chord |
| Doubling / octave guitar | `overdriven-guitar` | Optional *separate* track only if you want a different timbre |
| Bass | `electric-bass-pick` | Picked = grittier; `electric-bass-finger` for rounder |
| Drone / "Hammond" | `rock-organ` or `drawbar-organ` | Optional sustained organ drone for psychedelia |
| Drums | (GM percussion / channel 10) | Slow half-time groove from above |

### Power chords in MIDI
Write the power chord as a **single stacked-pitch note in one guitar track**:
`"C2+G2+C3"` = root, **+7 (perfect 5th)**, **+12 (octave)**, sounded together. This
reads as one chordal guitar part instead of several look-alike layer tracks. Double
the root with the **bass an octave below** in its own track (different instrument).
Only split into parallel tracks when a voice needs a *different instrument or rhythm*
(e.g. a separate clean octave guitar). Per-note `velocity` adds accent/weight.

### Settings checklist
- **BPM:** start at `65`–`75`.
- **Octave:** write riffs low — roots around **C2–C3** (this is your "down-tuning").
- **Key:** a minor key (C minor and E minor are idiomatic).
- **Durations:** favor `2` and `1` (halves/wholes) with occasional `4`/`8` for movement.
- **Rests / subtraction:** a note with `pitch "R"` (also `rest`/`-`) is true silence for
  its duration. This is how you get doom's negative space in the skill — rest the guitar
  layers out for a breather, leave the bass alone, then bring the wall back heavier. A
  leading rest also delays a voice's entrance for a slow build-in.

### Example: a C-minor main riff (Iommi shape)
One `distortion-guitar` track, each riff note voiced as a root+5th+octave power chord
(`"C2+G2+C3"`), ~70 BPM, 4/4 — pair with a bass an octave down (C1 …) for the full wall:

```
{"pitch": "C2+G2+C3",    "duration": "2", "velocity": 115}   # root, let it ring (half note)
{"pitch": "Eb2+Bb2+Eb3", "duration": "4"}                    # ♭3
{"pitch": "Gb2+Db3+Gb3", "duration": "2"}                    # ♭5 / tritone — hang on the tension
{"pitch": "F2+C3+F3",    "duration": "4"}                    # 4
{"pitch": "Eb2+Bb2+Eb3", "duration": "4"}                    # ♭3
{"pitch": "C2+G2+C3",    "duration": "1", "velocity": 115}   # home — whole note, let it bloom
```

That is one bar-and-a-half of the riff. **Cycle it 4–8 times**, then state a
variation (change only the tail), rest the guitar layers out to drop to bass alone,
and bring it back heavier.

## Quick-Start Recipe

1. Pick a **minor key** and set BPM **65–75**, felt in **half-time**.
2. Write **one riff** on the **Minor Blues scale**, low octave (root ~C2), built from a
   **pedal root + a tritone**. Keep it to a few notes with big durations.
3. **Power-chord it:** one guitar track, each note a stacked `root+5th+octave` chord
   (`"C2+G2+C3"`); double the root with bass −12 in its own track.
4. Lay a **half-time drum groove** (kick on 1, snare on 3, sparse washing ride).
5. **Cycle the riff 4–8 times** before changing anything.
6. Add **dynamics:** a stripped bass+drums breakdown, then the riff back heavier;
   optionally an **"even slower"** half-speed version for the climax.
7. Optional: **organ drone**, sparse reverbed vocals, a wah/lead jam over the drone.
8. **Let it run long** and **decay into feedback**. Don't rush. Trust the riff.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Too fast.** If it sounds urgent, it's not doom. Slow down.
- **Too many notes.** Three great notes beat thirty. Subtract.
- **No repetition.** Changing riffs constantly kills the trance — commit and cycle.
- **Resolving every phrase.** Leave the tritone/♭2 hanging. Tension is the point.
- **Fully scooped mids.** Sounds huge solo, disappears in the mix. Keep mids that cut.
- **Busy double-kick drumming.** Wrong genre energy; keep drums heavy and simple.
- **No dynamics.** All-loud-all-the-time is fatiguing. Use silence and subtraction.
- **Clean/clinical tone.** Without fuzz and warmth it's just slow rock, not doom.
